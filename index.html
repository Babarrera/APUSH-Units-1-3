Period 1: 1491-1607

American Pageant · Unit 1 Summary · Guided Notes

What are the 3cs this period covers? Contact, Commerce, Competing philosophies.

1. Native Societies Before European Contact

Description: Diverse, complex, and adapted to their environments.

Examples:
Aztecs: Urban (Tenochtitlan), centralized government, tribute systems.
Pueblos: Agricultural (maize), cliff dwellings, Southwest.
Eastern Woodlands: Matrilineal (Iroquois), hunter-gatherer & farming mix.
Key Point: Societies were not static—they evolved over time (e.g., Cahokia's decline pre-1492).

2. Major Effect of Columbus's Voyage (1492)

Cause and Effect: Triggered the Columbian Exchange → global transfer of goods, people, diseases.
Effect: Demographic catastrophe for Natives (disease), rise of transatlantic slavery, European dominance.

4. "Competing Philosophies"

What?: Ideological clashes over colonization:
Doctrine of Discovery (1493): Europeans claimed land by "divine right."
Bartolomé de las Casas: Argued Natives had souls; opposed brutality.
Religious Rivalry: Catholics (Spain) vs. Protestants (England) for souls/territory.

5. Skill Focus: Multiple Choice

Correct Answer: B (Growing dependence on indentured servants until 1700, then African slavery).
3 Tips: Analyze visuals first → identify trends. Eliminate absolutes. Context over dates.

Key Takeaways for Period 1: Natives were diverse; Columbian Exchange changed everything. Competing philosophies shaped colonization. Multiple-choice tests interpretation—practice with visuals!

Was religion important to early colonists? Yes. It dictated decisions. Many came to freely practice religion. Examples: Salem Witch Trials, Great Awakening.

What role does glory play? For monarchs, legacy; for colonists, creating self-sustaining communities. Conflict arose over how glory would be achieved and at what cost.

Review Period 2 timeline

  • 1607 – Jamestown, Virginia, Founded: First permanent English settlement; tobacco saved colony.
  • 1619 – Africans in the New World (Jamestown): First recorded Africans; set stage for race-based slavery.
  • 1620 – Pilgrims Land (Mayflower Compact): Self-government model; democratic governance.
  • 1626–1664 – New Amsterdam: Dutch trading hub; later New York.
  • 1630 – Boston: The City on the Hill: Puritan model society.

American Pageant Chapter 1 APUSH Review (Period 1)

  • Native Americans Pre-Contact: Migrated via Bering Strait; diverse societies adapted to environments.
  • Native American Culture: Animism; Pueblo irrigation; Iroquois Confederacy.

Guided Notes: APUSH Key Concept 1.1

Key Concept 1.1: "As native populations migrated and settled across the vast expanse of North America over time, they developed distinct and increasingly complex societies by adapting to and transforming their diverse environments."

Impact of Maize cultivation: Less hunting/gathering, population increase, permanent villages, Tenochtitlan (250,000).
Great Plains and Great Basin: Hunting/gathering, horse transformed bison hunting.
Northeast, Mississippi River Valley, and Atlantic Seaboard: Mixed hunting and agriculture; Iroquois matriarchal, burned forests.
Cahokia Indians: near St. Louis (30,000 people in 1200).

Key Concept 1.2

"Contact among Europeans, Native Americans, and Africans resulted in the Columbian Exchange and significant social, cultural, and political changes on both sides of the Atlantic Ocean."

Reasons for European exploration: Gold, silver, economic/military competition (glory), spread Christianity (Spain).
Technology: Sextant, compass, caravel; joint stock company.
Spanish Caste System: Peninsulares, Creoles, Mestizos/Mulattos.
Misunderstandings: Gender (matrilineal), land (communal), religion (animism).
Resistance & Debates: Sepúlveda (harsh treatment) vs. Las Casas (better treatment).

Labor, Slavery, and Caste in the Spanish Colonial System

  • Encomienda System: Forced Native labor on plantations/mines.
  • Mission System: Forced conversion and labor.
  • Atlantic Slave Trade: Increased as native population declined.
  • New Ethnicities: Mestizos, Mulattos.
  • Caste System (Castas): Rigid racial hierarchy.

Sepúlveda vs. Las Casas

Sepúlveda: Indians are "natural slaves" – intellectual inferiority, savage practices, lack of private property.
Las Casas: Indians are brothers – sophisticated societies, capacity for reason, shared humanity and Christian brotherhood.

Colonial Comparison Chart (New Mexico, New France, New Netherland, Jamestown)

  • New Mexico (1598): Find gold & convert Natives; Oñate's brutality at Acoma.
  • New France (1608): Fur trade; alliance with Huron.
  • New Netherland (1609): Fur trading; alliance with Iroquois (guns).
  • Chesapeake (Jamestown): Tobacco, headright system, Bacon's Rebellion → shift to slavery.
  • Puritanism / Mass. Bay: "City upon a hill"; freemen voting; self-governance.

APUSH Key Concept 2.2

Big Idea: British colonies developed a unique American identity through exchanges with Great Britain – both stronger bonds (continuity) and resistance (change).
Anglicization: adopting English norms. Mercantilism & Navigation Acts. Colonial resistance from self-government, Enlightenment, Great Awakening.
Rise of African slavery: large farmland, European demand, shortage of indentured servants after Bacon's Rebellion. Resistance: Stono Rebellion (1739), covert actions, maintaining family/culture.

Spanish, English, French, and Dutch Colonization (Periods 1 & 2) – Big picture

Spanish: extraction of precious metals, encomienda, caste system, Pueblo Revolt.
English: agriculture, representative assemblies, hostility toward Natives; regional differences (New England, Middle, Southern).
French: fur trade, alliances, intermarriage, no representative assembly.
Dutch: trade posts, joint-stock company, religious toleration.

The Regions of British Colonies (Chesapeake, New England, Middle, Southern)

  • Chesapeake: Tobacco, indentured servants → slavery, House of Burgesses.
  • New England: Puritans, town meetings, mixed economy, religious intolerance.
  • Middle: Diverse, religious toleration, cereal crops ("breadbasket").
  • Southern & West Indies: Plantation crops (rice, sugar), enslaved majority.

Atlantic Economy, Mercantilism, Navigation Acts

Slavery was "foundation" of British economy. Mercantilism: colonies exist for mother country. Navigation Acts: restricted trade to English ships/enumerated goods. Salutary neglect led to colonial autonomy.

The First Great Awakening

Definition: Religious revival (1730s-1770s), emotional personal faith.
Challenged: traditional religious authority, reaction against Enlightenment rationalism.
Key figures: Jonathan Edwards ("Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God"), George Whitefield (itinerant preacher).
Old Lights vs. New Lights: Traditionalists vs. revival supporters. Effects: increased denominations, undermined authority, unified colonies.

Growth of African Slavery – Stono Rebellion, Middle Passage, Slave Codes

  • Stono Rebellion (1739): Overt resistance in SC.
  • Middle Passage: brutal transatlantic voyage; high mortality.
  • Chattel slavery: status of mother determined enslavement; racial caste system.

Period 3: 1754-1800

3 themes: Conflict, Nation Building, and Expansion.

The Seven Years' War (French and Indian War)

Who: Britain (+ colonists) vs. France (+ Indian allies).
Cause: Ohio River Valley dispute.
Albany Conference (1754): Benjamin Franklin's Plan of Union (rejected).
Treaty of Paris (1763): Britain gained French Canada & land east of Mississippi; France removed from North America; Spain got Louisiana.
Effects: British debt → end of salutary neglect → new taxes; Proclamation of 1763; Pontiac's Rebellion.

Revolutionary War – Key Points

  • Lexington & Concord (1775): "shot heard round the world".
  • Common Sense (1776 – Thomas Paine): attacked monarchy, called for independence.
  • Declaration of Independence (July 4, 1776): Jefferson, natural rights (Locke), list of grievances.
  • British advantages: superior military, navy, wealth, Loyalists.
  • American advantages: home territory, Washington's leadership, ideological commitment, French alliance after Saratoga (1777).
  • Yorktown (1781): last major battle; surrender of Cornwallis.
  • Treaty of Paris (1783): recognized US independence, boundaries to Mississippi River.

Articles of Confederation (1781-1789)

Weaknesses: no power to tax, no executive/judicial, 9/13 to pass laws, unanimous to amend.
Accomplishments: won Revolutionary War, Northwest Ordinance (1787) – created process for new states, banned slavery in NW territory, funded education.
Shays' Rebellion (1786-87): farmers' revolt exposed weakness → led to Constitutional Convention.

Constitutional Convention (1787)

  • Virginia Plan: representation by population (large states).
  • New Jersey Plan: equal representation (small states).
  • Great Compromise: bicameral legislature – House by population, Senate equal.
  • 3/5 Compromise: enslaved counted as 3/5 for representation/taxation.
  • Slave Trade compromise: couldn't ban importation for 20 years.

Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists

  • Federalists (Hamilton, Madison, Jay): strong national government, loose construction, supported Constitution; wrote Federalist Papers.
  • Anti-Federalists (Patrick Henry, George Mason): weak central government, states' rights, demanded Bill of Rights.
  • Bill of Rights (1791): first 10 amendments – secured ratification.

Washington's Presidency (1789-1797)

  • Precedents: cabinet, two terms.
  • Hamilton's financial plan: assume state debts, national bank, whiskey tax, protective tariff.
  • Jefferson's opposition: strict construction, agrarian ideal.
  • Whiskey Rebellion (1794): federal government showed force.
  • Jay's Treaty (1795): British leave forts but no impressment solution; unpopular.
  • Pinckney's Treaty (1795): navigation rights on Mississippi, right of deposit at New Orleans.
  • Washington's Farewell Address: warned against political parties and permanent foreign alliances.

John Adams & the Rise of Political Parties

  • XYZ Affair (1797-98): French bribe demand → Quasi-War with France.
  • Alien and Sedition Acts (1798): restricted immigrants, criminalized criticism of government.
  • Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions (Jefferson & Madison): argued states could nullify unconstitutional laws.

Revolution of 1800

Election of 1800 – Jefferson (Democratic-Republican) defeated Adams (Federalist). First peaceful transfer of power between opposing parties in modern history.

DBQ Guide (Document Based Questions)

Format: 60 min total (15 plan, 45 write). 25% of exam score.
Thesis (1 pt): historically defensible, line of reasoning.
Contextualization (1 pt): 3-4 sentences about events before time period.
Evidence (3 pts): use at least 4 documents, describe + connect to thesis; 1 point for outside evidence.
Analysis (1 pt): source at least 2 documents using HIPP (Historical Situation, Intended Audience, Purpose, Point of View, Why).
Complexity (1 pt): use all 7 documents or source 4 documents.

Multiple Choice Tips

  • Read questions before source.
  • Read citation (author, date, source).
  • Eliminate absolutes.
  • No penalty for wrong answers – educated guess.

Short Answer Tips

  • 20% of exam score.
  • Answer all parts (A, B, C).
  • Support argument with evidence.
