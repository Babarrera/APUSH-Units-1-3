Period 1: 1491-1607

The 3 C's: Contact, Commerce, Competing philosophies.

1. Native Societies Before European Contact

Description: Diverse, complex, and adapted to their environments.

  • Aztecs: Urban (Tenochtitlan), centralized government, tribute systems.
  • Pueblos: Agricultural (maize), cliff dwellings, Southwest.
  • Eastern Woodlands: Matrilineal (Iroquois), hunter-gatherer & farming mix.

Key Point: Societies were not static—they evolved over time (e.g., Cahokia's decline pre-1492).

2. Major Effect of Columbus's Voyage (1492)

Cause and Effect: Triggered the Columbian Exchange → global transfer of goods, people, diseases.
Effect: Demographic catastrophe for Natives (disease), rise of transatlantic slavery, European dominance.

4. "Competing Philosophies"

What? Ideological clashes over colonization.

  • Doctrine of Discovery (1493): Europeans claimed land by "divine right."
  • Bartolomé de las Casas: Argued Natives had souls; opposed brutality.
  • Religious Rivalry: Catholics (Spain) vs. Protestants (England) for souls/territory.

Valladolid Debate:
Sepúlveda: Indians are "natural slaves" – intellectual inferiority, savage practices.
Las Casas: Indians are brothers – sophisticated societies, capacity for reason, shared humanity.

5. Spanish Labor & Caste Systems

  • Encomienda System: Forced Native labor on plantations/mines.
  • Mission System: Forced conversion and labor.
  • New Ethnicities: Mestizos, Mulattos.
  • Caste System (Castas): Rigid racial hierarchy (Peninsulares, Creoles, Mestizos/Mulattos).

6. Skill Focus: Multiple Choice

Correct Answer Example: B (Growing dependence on indentured servants until 1700, then African slavery).

3. Slavery & Labor Systems

  • Transatlantic Slave Trade (Middle Passage): Brutal voyage; high mortality.
  • Chattel Slavery: Status of mother determined enslavement; racial caste system.
  • Slave Resistance: Covert (breaking tools, feigning illness) & Overt (Stono Rebellion 1739).
  • Bacon's Rebellion (1676): Exposed class tensions; led planters to shift from indentured servants to African slavery.

4. Conflict & Interaction with Natives

  • Metacom's War (King Philip's War, 1675-76): Devastated Native power in New England.
  • Pueblo Revolt (1680): Successful uprising against Spanish in NM; forced Spanish to be more accommodating.

5. Ideas & Identity

  • The Enlightenment: Intellectual movement (Locke's natural rights). Undermined traditional authority; foundation for Revolution.
  • The First Great Awakening (1730s-70s): Religious revival (Edwards, Whitefield). Challenged authority, unified colonies, promoted spiritual equality.
  • Mercantilism & Salutary Neglect: Colonies exist for mother country. Lax enforcement allowed colonial self-government.

Period 3: 1754-1800

3 Themes: Conflict, Nation Building, and Expansion.

1. The Seven Years' War (French and Indian War)

Who: Britain (+colonists) vs. France (+Indian allies). Cause: Ohio River Valley dispute.
Albany Conference (1754): Ben Franklin's Plan of Union (rejected).
Treaty of Paris (1763): Britain gained French Canada & land east of Mississippi; France removed from North America.
Effects: British debt → end of salutary neglect → new taxes; Proclamation of 1763; Pontiac's Rebellion.

3. The Revolutionary War

  • Lexington & Concord (1775): "Shot heard round the world."
  • Common Sense (1776 - Thomas Paine): Attacked monarchy; called for independence in plain language.
  • Declaration of Independence (July 4, 1776): Jefferson; natural rights (Locke); social contract; list of grievances.
  • British Advantages: Superior military, navy, wealth, Loyalists.
  • American Advantages: Home territory, Washington's leadership, ideological commitment, French alliance after Saratoga (1777).
  • Yorktown (1781): Last major battle; surrender of Cornwallis.
  • Treaty of Paris (1783): Recognized US independence, boundaries to Mississippi River.

4. Articles of Confederation (1781-1789)

Weaknesses: No power to tax, no executive/judicial, 9/13 to pass laws, unanimous to amend.
Accomplishments: Won Revolutionary War, Northwest Ordinance (1787) – created process for new states, banned slavery in NW territory, funded education.
Shays' Rebellion (1786-87): Farmers' revolt exposed weakness → led to Constitutional Convention.

5. Constitutional Convention (1787)

  • Virginia Plan: Representation by population (large states).
  • New Jersey Plan: Equal representation (small states).
  • Great Compromise: Bicameral legislature – House by population, Senate equal.
  • 3/5 Compromise: Enslaved counted as 3/5 for representation/taxation.
  • Slave Trade Compromise: Couldn't ban importation for 20 years.

6. Ratification Debate: Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists

  • Federalists (Hamilton, Madison, Jay): Strong national government, loose construction; wrote Federalist Papers.
  • Anti-Federalists (Patrick Henry, George Mason): Weak central government, states' rights, demanded Bill of Rights.
  • Bill of Rights (1791): First 10 amendments – secured ratification.

7. Washington's Presidency (1789-1797)

  • Precedents: Cabinet, two terms.
  • Hamilton's Financial Plan: Assume state debts, national bank, whiskey tax, protective tariff.
  • Jefferson's Opposition: Strict construction, agrarian ideal.
  • Whiskey Rebellion (1794): Federal government showed force.
  • Jay's Treaty (1795): British leave forts but no impressment solution; unpopular.
  • Pinckney's Treaty (1795): Navigation rights on Mississippi.
  • Washington's Farewell Address: Warned against political parties and permanent foreign alliances.

8. John Adams & the Rise of Parties

  • XYZ Affair (1797-98): French bribe demand → Quasi-War with France.
  • Alien and Sedition Acts (1798): Restricted immigrants, criminalized criticism of government.
  • Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions (Jefferson & Madison): Argued states could nullify unconstitutional laws (states' rights).

9. Revolution of 1800

Election of 1800: Jefferson (Democratic-Republican) defeated Adams (Federalist).
Significance: First peaceful transfer of power between opposing parties in modern history.

Study Notes – Historical Context

What tighter control? After Seven Years' War, Britain ended salutary neglect: Proclamation 1763, new taxes (Sugar, Stamp), enforcement of trade laws.
Ideas of self-government: Local traditions (colonial assemblies), British precedent (English liberties), Enlightenment (Locke).
Colonial representation: Colonists believed in actual representation; Britain argued virtual representation.
Calls for resistance: Rights of British subjects, rights of the individual, local self-rule, Enlightenment ideas.

Video-Based Questions – Key Points

  • Boycotts were collective refusals to buy British goods; they hurt British merchants and forced repeal.
  • Intolerable Acts: Closed Boston Harbor, revoked MA charter, quartering troops. Colonial response: First Continental Congress, militias.
  • Common Sense attacked monarchy, written in plain language, sold 150,000+ copies, turned public opinion toward independence.

AP Skills Bootcamp & Exam Strategies

DBQ Guide (Document Based Questions)

  • Format: 60 min total (15 plan, 45 write). 25% of exam score.
  • Thesis (1 pt): historically defensible, line of reasoning.
  • Contextualization (1 pt): 3-4 sentences about events before time period.
  • Evidence (3 pts): use at least 4 documents, describe + connect to thesis; 1 point for outside evidence.
  • Analysis (1 pt): source at least 2 documents using HIPP (Historical Situation, Intended Audience, Purpose, Point of View).
  • Complexity (1 pt): use all 7 documents or source 4 documents.

LEQ & Short Answer Tips

  • No penalty for wrong answers – educated guess.
  • Plan before you write. A good plan = a good essay.
  • Thesis formula: "Although X, because A and B, therefore Y." (X = counter-argument, A/B = evidence, Y = argument).

Historical Thinking Skills

  • Causation: Why did something happen?
  • Continuity and Change: How did things change and/or remain the same?
  • Comparison: Compare and contrast events, people, or ideas.

Additional Quick Review – The Big Ideas

  • Articles of Confederation: Too weak.
  • Constitution: Stronger, with compromises (Great, 3/5, Commerce).
  • Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists: Debate over ratification and power.
  • Washington: Set precedents (Cabinet, 2-terms, Farewell Address).
  • Hamilton vs. Jefferson: Their debate created the first two parties.
  • Election of 1800: Peaceful transfer of power = "Revolution."
