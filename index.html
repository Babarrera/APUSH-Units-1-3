📍 Period 1: 1491–1607

What are the 3 C's this period covers? Contact, Commerce, Competing philosophies.

1. Native Societies Before European Contact

Description: Diverse, complex, and adapted to their environments.
Examples: Aztecs (Urban Tenochtitlan, centralized government, tribute systems); Pueblos (Agricultural maize, cliff dwellings, Southwest); Eastern Woodlands (Matrilineal Iroquois, hunter-gatherer & farming mix).
Key Point: Societies were not static—they evolved over time (e.g., Cahokia's decline pre-1492).

Additional details from American Pageant / Guided Notes:
Key Concept 1.1: "As native populations migrated and settled across the vast expanse of North America over time, they developed distinct and increasingly complex societies by adapting to and transforming their diverse environments."
Impact of Maize cultivation: Less hunting/gathering, population increase, permanent villages, Tenochtitlan (250,000).
Great Plains and Great Basin: Hunting/gathering, horse transformed bison hunting.
Northeast, Mississippi River Valley, and Atlantic Seaboard: Mixed hunting and agriculture; Iroquois matriarchal, burned forests.
Cahokia Indians: near St. Louis (30,000 people in 1200).
Native American Culture: Animism; Pueblo irrigation; Iroquois Confederacy.
Pre-Contact Migration: Migrated via Bering Strait; diverse societies adapted to environments.

2. Major Effect of Columbus's Voyage (1492)

Cause and Effect: Triggered the Columbian Exchange → global transfer of goods, people, diseases.
Effect: Demographic catastrophe for Natives (disease), rise of transatlantic slavery, European dominance.

4. "Competing Philosophies"

What? Ideological clashes over colonization:
Doctrine of Discovery (1493): Europeans claimed land by "divine right."
Bartolomé de las Casas: Argued Natives had souls; opposed brutality.
Religious Rivalry: Catholics (Spain) vs. Protestants (England) for souls/territory.
Valladolid Debates (Las Casas vs. Sepúlveda):
Sepúlveda: Indians are "natural slaves" – intellectual inferiority, savage practices, lack of private property.
Las Casas: Indians are brothers – sophisticated societies, capacity for reason, shared humanity and Christian brotherhood.

5. Spanish Colonization & Labor Systems

Reasons for European exploration: Gold, silver, economic/military competition (glory), spread Christianity (Spain).
Technology: Sextant, compass, caravel; joint stock company.
Encomienda System: Forced Native labor on plantations/mines.
Mission System: Forced conversion and labor.
Atlantic Slave Trade: Increased as native population declined.
New Ethnicities: Mestizos, Mulattos.
Spanish Caste System (Castas): Rigid racial hierarchy – Peninsulares, Creoles, Mestizos/Mulattos.
Misunderstandings: Gender (matrilineal), land (communal), religion (animism).
Resistance & Debates: Sepúlveda (harsh treatment) vs. Las Casas (better treatment).
Pueblo Revolt (1680): Successful uprising against Spanish.

What are the 3 G's? Gold, God, Glory.

Was religion important to early colonists? Yes. It dictated decisions. Many came to freely practice religion. Examples: Salem Witch Trials, Great Awakening.
What role does glory play? For monarchs, legacy; for colonists, creating self-sustaining communities. Conflict arose over how glory would be achieved and at what cost.

2. List the 7 historical themes

1. Identity; 2. Work, exchange, and technology; 3. Peopling; 4. Politics and power; 5. America and the world; 6. Environment and geography; 7. Ideas, belief, and culture.

4. Review Period 2 timeline

  • 1607 – Jamestown, Virginia, Founded: First permanent English settlement; tobacco saved colony.
  • 1619 – Africans in the New World (Jamestown): First recorded Africans; set stage for race-based slavery.
  • 1620 – Pilgrims Land (Mayflower Compact): Self-government model; democratic governance.
  • 1626–1664 – New Amsterdam: Dutch trading hub; later New York.
  • 1630 – Boston: The City on the Hill: Puritan model society.

5. Spanish, English, French, and Dutch Colonization – Big picture

Spanish: extraction of precious metals, encomienda, caste system, Pueblo Revolt.
English: agriculture, representative assemblies, hostility toward Natives; regional differences (New England, Middle, Southern).
French: fur trade, alliances, intermarriage, no representative assembly.
Dutch: trade posts, joint-stock company, religious toleration.

7. Atlantic Economy, Mercantilism, Navigation Acts

Slavery was "foundation" of British economy. Mercantilism: colonies exist for mother country. Navigation Acts: restricted trade to English ships/enumerated goods. Salutary neglect led to colonial autonomy.

8. The First Great Awakening

Definition: Religious revival (1730s-1770s), emotional personal faith. Challenged: traditional religious authority, reaction against Enlightenment rationalism. Key figures: Jonathan Edwards ("Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God"), George Whitefield (itinerant preacher). Old Lights vs. New Lights: Traditionalists vs. revival supporters. Effects: increased denominations, undermined authority, unified colonies.

9. Growth of African Slavery – Stono Rebellion, Middle Passage, Slave Codes

Stono Rebellion (1739): Overt resistance in SC. Middle Passage: brutal transatlantic voyage; high mortality. Chattel slavery: status of mother determined enslavement; racial caste system.

APUSH Key Concept 2.2 (Big Idea): British colonies developed a unique American identity through exchanges with Great Britain – both stronger bonds (continuity) and resistance (change).
Anglicization: adopting English norms. Mercantilism & Navigation Acts. Colonial resistance from self-government, Enlightenment, Great Awakening.
Rise of African slavery: large farmland, European demand, shortage of indentured servants after Bacon's Rebellion.
Resistance: Stono Rebellion (1739), covert actions, maintaining family/culture.

11. Metacom's War / King Philip's War (1675-1676)

A major conflict between New England colonists and a coalition of Native American tribes led by Metacom (King Philip). Extremely bloody war that devastated Native American power in New England. Symbolized the ultimate failure of coexistence and the relentless pressure of colonial expansion on Native lands.

12. The Enlightenment

Intellectual movement emphasizing reason, science, and individual rights (e.g., John Locke's natural rights). Spread ideas about liberty and self-government that colonists would later use to challenge British authority. Undermined traditional authority (like the monarchy) and provided a philosophical foundation for the American Revolution.

📍 Period 3: 1754–1800

3 themes: Conflict, Nation Building, and Expansion.

1. The Seven Years' War (French and Indian War)

Who: Britain (+ colonists) vs. France (+ Indian allies). Cause: Ohio River Valley dispute. Albany Conference (1754): Benjamin Franklin's Plan of Union (rejected). Treaty of Paris (1763): Britain gained French Canada & land east of Mississippi; France removed from North America; Spain got Louisiana. Effects: British debt → end of salutary neglect → new taxes; Proclamation of 1763; Pontiac's Rebellion.

3. Revolutionary War – Key Points

Lexington & Concord (1775): "shot heard round the world".
Common Sense (1776 – Thomas Paine): attacked monarchy, called for independence.
Declaration of Independence (July 4, 1776): Jefferson, natural rights (Locke), list of grievances.
British advantages: superior military, navy, wealth, Loyalists.
American advantages: home territory, Washington's leadership, ideological commitment, French alliance after Saratoga (1777).
Yorktown (1781): last major battle; surrender of Cornwallis.
Treaty of Paris (1783): recognized US independence, boundaries to Mississippi River.

4. Articles of Confederation (1781-1789)

Weaknesses: no power to tax, no executive/judicial, 9/13 to pass laws, unanimous to amend.
Accomplishments: won Revolutionary War, Northwest Ordinance (1787) – created process for new states, banned slavery in NW territory, funded education.
Shays' Rebellion (1786-87): farmers' revolt exposed weakness → led to Constitutional Convention.

5. Constitutional Convention (1787)

Virginia Plan: representation by population (large states). New Jersey Plan: equal representation (small states). Great Compromise: bicameral legislature – House by population, Senate equal. 3/5 Compromise: enslaved counted as 3/5 for representation/taxation. Slave Trade compromise: couldn't ban importation for 20 years.

6. Federalists vs. Anti-Federalists

Federalists (Hamilton, Madison, Jay): strong national government, loose construction, supported Constitution; wrote Federalist Papers.
Anti-Federalists (Patrick Henry, George Mason): weak central government, states' rights, demanded Bill of Rights.
Bill of Rights (1791): first 10 amendments – secured ratification.

7. Washington's Presidency (1789-1797)

Precedents: cabinet, two terms. Hamilton's financial plan: assume state debts, national bank, whiskey tax, protective tariff. Jefferson's opposition: strict construction, agrarian ideal. Whiskey Rebellion (1794): federal government showed force. Jay's Treaty (1795): British leave forts but no impressment solution; unpopular. Pinckney's Treaty (1795): navigation rights on Mississippi, right of deposit at New Orleans. Washington's Farewell Address: warned against political parties and permanent foreign alliances.

8. John Adams & the Rise of Political Parties

XYZ Affair (1797-98): French bribe demand → Quasi-War with France. Alien and Sedition Acts (1798): restricted immigrants, criminalized criticism of government. Kentucky and Virginia Resolutions (Jefferson & Madison): argued states could nullify unconstitutional laws.

9. Revolution of 1800

Election of 1800 – Jefferson (Democratic-Republican) defeated Adams (Federalist). First peaceful transfer of power between opposing parties in modern history.

10. Calls for resistance based on 4 ideas

1. Rights of British subjects – "no taxation without representation" (Patrick Henry's Stamp Act Resolutions).
2. The rights of the individual – Enlightenment natural rights (James Otis).
3. Local traditions of self-rule – Stamp Act Congress, First Continental Congress.
4. The ideas of the Enlightenment – Republicanism, John Locke's social contract.

11. British Response (Intolerable Acts) vs. Colonial Response

British: Closed Boston Harbor, revoked MA charter, allowed quartering in private homes.
Colonial: Sent supplies to Boston, formed First Continental Congress, organized militias and "Minutemen."

12. Key Points from Common Sense

Attacked monarchy and hereditary rule. Argued it was "common sense" for a continent to not be ruled by a distant island. Said America's cause was the cause of all mankind. Sold 150,000+ copies; pivotal in turning public opinion toward independence.

13. The "Critical Period" (1781-1789)

Articles of Confederation.

14. The Revolutionary "Mindset" – Why did they rebel?

End of Salutary Neglect; "No Taxation Without Representation"; Enlightenment Ideas (Locke); Local Self-Rule.

15. Who Were the People?

Patriots: (~40-45%) Supported independence. Loyalists (Tories): (~20-30%) Remained loyal to Crown. Neutral: The remainder.
African Americans: Fought on both sides; British promised freedom. Native Americans: Mostly sided with British; biggest losers. Women: Republican Motherhood – raising virtuous, educated sons.

16. The Evolution of Government: From Confederation to Constitution

Articles of Confederation = Fear of a powerful tyrant → weak government.
U.S. Constitution = Fear of chaos (Shays' Rebellion) → stronger, balanced government with Separation of Powers and Checks and Balances.

21. Key Terms & Concepts (Additional Resources)

Salutary Neglect: British policy of avoiding strict enforcement of parliamentary laws.
Virtual Representation: British theory that Parliament represented all British subjects.
Actual Representation: Colonists' belief that they could only be taxed by their own elected representatives.
Republican Motherhood: Women's responsibility to raise patriotic, educated children for the republic.
